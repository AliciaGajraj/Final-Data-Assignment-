Outdoor Dining, A New Norm in New York City

By: Alicia Gajraj

December 17th, 2021

The pandemic affected so many businesses in New York City, specifically food service establishments. These establishments were forced to close down because of the rising infection rates. However, to keep their business afloat, many food service establishments implemented outdoor dining.

Now, New York City is filled with outdoor dinning options in different boroughs. Customers have the option to either sit inside or outside under certain restrictions.

According to the New York City Department of Transportation, "establishments seeking to utilize additional public sidewalk or roadway space for outdoor dining operation must apply online at nyc.gov/openrestaurants and may commerce upon self-certification." The Open Resaurant Application was proposed in June 2020 to help food service establishments. Under Phase Two of New York Forward plan, it allowed food service establishments to utilize any privately owned outdoor space, such as front yards, or courtyards, open air roof top space, balconies, terraces, private parking lots, etc. It was specifically designed for food services to apply for sidewalk access, roadway access or both.

In total, there were 7,122 approved applications for sidewalk seating. Throughout the five boroughs, Manhattan has the highest number of approved sidewalk seating followed by Brooklyn, Queens, Bronx and Staten Island.

There were 5,080 approved applications for roadway seating. Throughout the five boroughs, Manhattan has the highest number of approved roadway seating followed by Brooklyn, Queens, Bronx and Staten Island.

Throughout the different boroughs, many businesses applied for both outdoor dining options. Only some establishments were approved for both roadway and sidewalk seating. There were 4,314 approved seating for both roadway and sidewalk seating. Manhattan has the highest number of both seating approval, followed by Brooklyn, Queens, Bronx and Staten Island.

Andrea Strong, the executive director for ROARny , fights for unemployed restaurant workers and their rights when facing financial hardships. "We made sure that outdoor dinning was made permanent, which meant that restaurants were going to be able to employ their restaurant workers for the winter," Strong said.
